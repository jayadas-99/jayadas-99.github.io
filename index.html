The Trigger — A measurement gap nobody had named

Brand teams were manually checking whether their products appeared in AI-generated answers. It was anecdotal, inconsistent, and unscalable. No one had a number. Nobody knew if a competitor was being referenced more. The problem existed but wasn't instrumented — which is exactly the kind of gap I've spent my career finding.

The Build vs Buy Decision

Three external vendors were evaluated. All offered solid monitoring but charged per-site licensing — at 400+ websites, the cost was prohibitive. More importantly, they couldn't be shaped around our brand taxonomy or internal workflows. I made the call to build an internal MVP in 8 weeks, pilot with 10 brands, then scale.

What I Owned

PRD, milestone planning, roadmap, KPI framework, stakeholder interviews with brand team leads, engineering team management, and MVP definition. This was the full PM loop on a product that didn't exist six months ago — initiated, owned, and shipped by me while holding a Software Engineer title.

What's Next

Scaling from pilot brands to full 400+ website coverage, adding prompt library customisation per brand category, and building a trend layer that tracks visibility changes over time as AI models update their training data.

Case Study

Paysa Pulse — the thinking behind it.

Why I removed 70% of the features, focused on 3 categories, and what that taught me about product decisions.

Try Paysa Pulse live ↗

Insight #1 · Indians think in salary cycles, not calendar months

Every Indian finance app shows a January 1–31 view. But nobody gets paid on January 1st. Salaried Indians think from the 1st to the 27th — or the 5th to the 4th. I built the entire UX around 4 phases of that cycle: Flush, Steady, Careful, Survival.

Insight #2 · Most spending categories are outside your control

Rent is fixed. EMIs are contractual. The only categories where habit actually changes the number are food delivery, quick commerce, and subscriptions. I removed everything else from the main view — deliberately.

Insight #3 · The upload step was the real blocker

The entire app is useless until the user successfully uploads a bank statement. I identified this as the activation problem — not a feature problem. The solution wasn't more features. It was fixing the empty state, adding demo data, and making the upload less fragile.

The Privacy Decision

PDF parsing happens locally in the browser via PDF.js. Only anonymised transaction text, with account numbers stripped, is ever sent to the AI. The file itself never leaves the device.

Paysa Pulse: a cleaner, category-first habit coach for spenders who want one clear path to better financial behaviour.

PRD Odyssey · Bristol Myers Squibb · May 2026

Problem Statement

Brand teams had no reliable way to know whether their brands appeared in AI-generated answers. As users increasingly use ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews instead of traditional search, visibility strategies built entirely around Google SEO became insufficient. The problem wasn't being measured — which meant it wasn't being managed.

User Persona — "Neha", Brand Manager

Responsible for 3–8 brand websites. Current workflow: manually types brand queries into ChatGPT, screenshots results, shares in a deck. Pain: no consistency, no trend data, no competitive context, no actionable output. Goal: know where the brand stands in AI answers and what to do about it.

North Star Metric

Recommendation Adoption Rate — the % of Odyssey-generated recommendations implemented by brand teams. A high adoption rate means output is trusted and specific enough to act on. A dashboard nobody acts on is a vanity product.

What I Cut from MVP & Why

Automated content rewriting was requested early. I pushed it to P2 — brand teams need to trust the diagnosis before they trust the prescription. Shipping recommendations they understand builds trust. Shipping AI-written content they didn't ask for breaks it.

Retrospective Odyssey · Honest version

Lock the North Star before writing the PRD

For the first three weeks, the team was building toward "visibility" as a vague goal. I defined the KPI framework later than I should have, which meant some early architectural decisions had to be revisited. North Star first, everything else second.

Pilot with skeptics, not just engaged teams

The 10 pilot brands were teams I knew were motivated. High adoption early felt good — but masked usability problems that only surfaced at broader rollout. I should have deliberately included one skeptical team. Their friction would have been more valuable than the enthusiasts' praise.

Define "recommendation" precisely at the start

First batch of recommendations were too vague: "improve your structured data." Brand managers forwarded to content teams who asked "which pages?" That translation layer cost two weeks. URL-specific recommendations should have been in the spec from day one.

Retrospective Paysa Pulse · Honest version

Talk to users before writing code

I built the first version based on my own frustration with Indian finance apps. Legitimate starting point — but not a substitute for interviews. The salary-cycle insight was real. The 3-category focus came later, from looking at actual transaction data. Eight user interviews upfront would have gotten me there in weeks instead of months of building and cutting.

Design backward from the activation moment

I thought "user uploads statement" was activation. It isn't. The real activation moment is "user sees a nudge that surprises them about their own spending." I should have designed backward from that moment, not forward from the upload step.

Remove at the spec stage, not after building

Goals, Badges, Share Card, Help Page — I built all of these before deciding they didn't belong. A sharper MoSCoW prioritisation, informed by a clearer persona, would have kept the initial scope tight. Shipping less faster beats shipping more and then cutting.
